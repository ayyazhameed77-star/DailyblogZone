The Beginner's Guide

10 Blogging Mistakes Every Beginner Makes — And How to Fix Them

By Alex Morgan May 28, 2026 12 min read

Everyone starts somewhere. The first post, the empty dashboard, the blinking cursor — and the overwhelming question: am I doing this right? Probably not entirely. And that's okay. What matters is knowing which mistakes are silently killing your blog before it even gets a chance to grow.

Whether you launched last week or last year, these are the ten missteps almost every new blogger stumbles into — and exactly what to do instead.

01
Strategy

Writing for Everyone — and Reaching No One

When you try to appeal to every possible reader, your voice becomes diluted, generic, and forgettable. "General lifestyle blog" is not a niche. Readers follow blogs because they feel understood — and you can't make someone feel understood when you're shouting into a crowd.

The Fix

Define your ideal reader as one specific person. What do they do? What do they struggle with? Write every post directly to that person, and your whole blog becomes magnetic.

02
Consistency

Publishing in Bursts, Then Disappearing

Three posts in one weekend, then silence for six weeks. This pattern confuses readers, tanks your SEO, and is impossible to sustain. Blogging is a marathon, not a series of sprints.

The Fix

Choose a publishing schedule you can realistically maintain — even if that's just once every two weeks. Boring consistency always outperforms brilliant inconsistency.

03
SEO

Ignoring Search Intent Completely

You can write beautifully and still get zero traffic if nobody is actually searching for what you wrote. Writing without any awareness of how real people phrase their questions online is one of the most common — and costly — beginner oversights.

The Fix

Before writing, type your topic into Google. Look at the autocomplete suggestions and the "People Also Ask" section. These reveal exactly how your audience talks and what they want to know.

"Your blog doesn't fail because you aren't talented enough. It fails because nobody told you the rules of the game."
— The Craft of Blogging
04
Readability

Writing Walls of Text

Online readers scan before they read. If they land on your post and see a dense, unbroken block of text, most will leave in under three seconds. The way your post looks is almost as important as what it says.

The Fix

Use short paragraphs (2–4 sentences max), meaningful subheadings every 300 words or so, and bullet points for any list of three or more items. White space is not wasted space — it's breathing room.

05
Voice

Sounding Like a Wikipedia Article

Stiff, overly formal writing is the fastest way to lose a reader. People come to blogs for a human voice — opinion, personality, even a little imperfection. If your prose could have been written by a committee, it's time to loosen up.

The Fix

Read your drafts aloud. Anything that sounds unnatural when spoken needs to be rewritten. Contractions, short sentences, even starting a sentence with "And" — all allowed. Write like you talk, then polish it.

06
Growth

Skipping an Email List in the Early Days

Social platforms change algorithms, get banned, or fall out of fashion. An email list is the only audience you truly own. Beginners who wait until they "have enough readers" to start building one almost always regret it — every email address not collected is one that's gone forever.

The Fix

Set up a simple email signup on Day 1, even with zero subscribers. Offer a small, useful freebie (a checklist, a short guide) in exchange for signing up. Start building before you think you need to.

07
Design

Neglecting Mobile Readers

More than 60% of web traffic comes from mobile devices. If your blog looks broken, cramped, or unreadable on a phone, you are failing the majority of your audience before they read a single word.

The Fix

Always preview every post on your phone before publishing. Choose a responsive theme and test it on at least two screen sizes. Font size on mobile should be no smaller than 16px.

08
Headlines

Writing Weak, Vague Post Titles

"My Thoughts on Productivity" will never compete with "7 Counterintuitive Habits That Helped Me Work Half the Hours." The title is the first — and often only — thing people use to decide whether to click. A weak title dooms a brilliant post.

The Fix

Write at least five title options for every post and choose the most specific, benefit-driven one. Ask yourself: "Does this title make someone feel like they'd be missing out if they didn't read this?"

09
Metrics

Obsessing Over Traffic Too Early

Checking Google Analytics every day when you have 12 posts published is a recipe for demoralization. Early traffic is almost always low — that's normal, not a sign of failure. Treating small numbers as evidence to quit is one of the main reasons promising blogs die young.

The Fix

For your first six months, focus on output, not outcomes. Your job is to write 30 good posts, develop your voice, and learn what resonates. Traffic comes after the foundation is built, not before.

10
Mindset

Waiting Until It's Perfect to Publish

Perfectionism is procrastination wearing a respectable costume. Beginners often sit on drafts for weeks, endlessly tweaking, convinced the post isn't ready. Meanwhile, an imperfect post that's live is infinitely more useful than a perfect one that exists only on your hard drive.

The Fix

Adopt a "good enough to help someone" standard instead of a "perfect" one. Publish, get feedback, improve. Your tenth post will be better than your first not because you waited, but because you shipped nine before it.

Now Go Make Mistakes — The Useful Kind

Every successful blogger you admire made every single one of these errors. The difference between them and the bloggers who gave up is simple: they kept going anyway, and they paid attention to what wasn't working.

Print this list. Tape it above your desk if you have to. Not to intimidate yourself, but as a reminder that these mistakes are fixable — every last one of them — and that recognizing them puts you already ahead of where most beginners are.

Now close this tab and go write something. Imperfectly, honestly, and for the right person.
