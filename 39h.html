The Vitamins Your

Long before science gave them names and numbers, people understood that certain foods made them strong while others left them withering. Sailors on long voyages lost their teeth and strength to scurvy — until someone discovered that a lemon could save their lives. Children in sunless cities bent their bones to rickets — until cod liver oil straightened them. These were not mysteries. They were deficiencies. Missing vitamins. And the lesson that history wrote in suffering is one that modern nutrition has now confirmed with extraordinary precision: your body is a magnificent machine, but it cannot manufacture most of what it needs to run. You must give it the right vitamins — every single day — or the machine begins, quietly and invisibly, to break down.

Vit A Vitamin A — The Vision and Skin Protector

Vitamin A is the guardian of your vision, your skin, and your immune barriers. It maintains the health of the epithelial cells that line every surface of your body — your eyes, your skin, your lungs, your gut — forming the physical walls that keep pathogens out. Without adequate Vitamin A, night blindness is one of the first symptoms to appear, followed by dry, rough skin and a dramatically increased susceptibility to infection. You find it in its most potent form in liver, eggs, and dairy — and in its plant-based precursor, beta-carotene, in the brilliant oranges and yellows of carrots, sweet potatoes, mangoes, and pumpkin. Eat the colours of sunrise and your eyes will thank you for it.

Best Food Sources

Liver, eggs, whole milk, carrots, sweet potato, butternut squash, mango, spinach, and kale. One medium sweet potato provides more than your entire daily requirement of Vitamin A in a single serving.

B12 Vitamin B12 — The Energy and Brain Vitamin

Vitamin B12 is the vitamin that keeps your nervous system alive and your mind sharp. It is essential for the production of red blood cells, the synthesis of DNA, and the maintenance of the myelin sheath — the protective coating around your nerve fibres that allows electrical signals to travel through the body at lightning speed. A B12 deficiency begins subtly — fatigue that will not lift, a strange tingling in the hands and feet, a mental fogginess that no amount of sleep resolves. Left unaddressed, it progresses to nerve damage, memory loss, and irreversible neurological harm. It is found almost exclusively in animal products — meat, fish, eggs, and dairy — making it a critical consideration for anyone following a plant-based diet, who should supplement without hesitation.

Who Needs Extra Attention

Vegans, vegetarians, adults over 50 (whose stomach acid decreases with age, impairing B12 absorption), and anyone taking long-term metformin or antacids should monitor B12 levels closely and consider supplementation under medical guidance.

Vit C Vitamin C — The Immune Warrior and Healer

Vitamin C may be the most famous vitamin in the world — and its reputation is entirely deserved. It is a powerful antioxidant that neutralises the free radicals that damage cells and accelerate ageing. It is essential for collagen synthesis — the structural protein that holds your skin, joints, tendons, and blood vessels together. Without it, wounds cannot heal, blood vessels become fragile, and teeth loosen from their roots. This is what scurvy looks like. But Vitamin C does far more than prevent catastrophe. It actively stimulates the production and function of white blood cells, shortens the duration of colds, and reduces the severity of respiratory infections. A single large red bell pepper contains twice the daily recommended intake. An orange. A kiwi. A cup of strawberries. This vitamin is everywhere in nature — and it wants to protect you.

Daily Dose of Vitamin C

Adults need 65–90 mg daily, with an upper safe limit of 2,000 mg. You can reach your daily requirement easily with one orange, half a red bell pepper, or a handful of strawberries. Unlike fat-soluble vitamins, any excess Vitamin C is simply flushed from the body — making it one of the safest vitamins to consume generously through food.

Vit D Vitamin D — The Sunshine Vitamin Your Bones Crave

Vitamin D is unlike any other vitamin — because your skin can manufacture it from sunlight. Fifteen to thirty minutes of midday sun on bare skin several times a week is enough for most people in sunny climates. But in the modern world — of offices, indoor living, sunscreen, and winters without light — deficiency has become extraordinarily widespread. Vitamin D is essential for calcium absorption and bone mineralisation, which is why children without it develop rickets and adults develop osteoporosis. But its role reaches far beyond bone. Vitamin D receptors exist in nearly every cell in the human body — in the brain, the heart, the immune system, and the pancreas. Low Vitamin D levels are linked to depression, increased cancer risk, autoimmune disease, and poor immune response to infection. Step into the light. Eat fatty fish, egg yolks, and fortified foods. And if you live far from the sun, supplement with confidence.

Vit E Vitamin E — The Cell Shield and Skin Glow Vitamin

Vitamin E is one of the body's most important fat-soluble antioxidants, working tirelessly inside cell membranes to prevent oxidative damage from destroying healthy tissue. It protects the fats in your cell walls from going rancid — a process called lipid peroxidation — that, unchecked, contributes to heart disease, cognitive decline, and premature ageing. Vitamin E also supports immune function, helps prevent blood clotting, and is one of the key nutrients responsible for healthy, glowing skin from the inside out. It is richest in nuts and seeds — particularly almonds and sunflower seeds — as well as in wheat germ oil, avocado, and leafy greens. A small handful of almonds each morning delivers your entire daily requirement of this quietly essential protector.

Vit K Vitamin K — The Clotting and Bone-Strengthening Secret

Vitamin K is the vitamin most people have never thought about — and one of the most consequential. It exists in two main forms: K1, found in leafy green vegetables and essential for blood clotting, and K2, found in fermented foods and certain animal products, which directs calcium into bones and teeth rather than into arteries. Without adequate Vitamin K, even the smallest cut would not stop bleeding. And without K2 in particular, the calcium you consume goes to the wrong places — hardening arteries instead of strengthening bones. Eating dark leafy greens daily — kale, spinach, broccoli, Swiss chard — ensures a generous supply of K1. Adding fermented foods like natto, aged cheese, and sauerkraut provides K2. Together, they guard your cardiovascular system and your skeleton silently and faithfully every single day.

The K2 and D3 Partnership

Vitamins D3 and K2 work in powerful synergy — D3 increases calcium absorption from the gut while K2 ensures that calcium is deposited into bones, not arteries. If you supplement with Vitamin D3, consider pairing it with K2 for optimal cardiovascular and bone protection. Always consult your doctor before beginning supplementation.

A Word of Wisdom

Vitamins obtained through whole, real food are always superior to supplements — because food delivers vitamins alongside thousands of co-nutrients, fibre, and phytochemicals that work together in ways no capsule can replicate. Supplement wisely where diet falls short, but build your vitamin foundation on a colourful, varied, whole-food plate first and always.

Your body asks for so little and gives so much in return. A carrot for Vitamin A. A sliver of salmon for D and B12. A handful of almonds for E. A cup of spinach for K. An orange for C. These are not sacrifices — they are gifts. Small, daily, delicious gifts you give to the most extraordinary machine ever created. Feed it the vitamins it cannot make alone, and it will reward you with energy, clarity, resilience, and a length of healthy life that no medicine can manufacture and no technology can replicate. The power, as it has always been, is on your plate.
