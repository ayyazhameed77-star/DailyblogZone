A World in One Country

Cameroon is often called "Africa in Miniature"—and for good reason. From the thundering Atlantic coastline to the misty peaks of West Africa's highest mountain, from steamy rainforests to Sahelian savannas, this bilingual nation packs an entire continent's diversity into one triangular territory. Straddling both English and French-speaking Africa, Cameroon is a cultural crossroads where over 250 ethnic groups and languages create a vibrant, harmonious chaos that captivates every traveler lucky enough to visit.

The Tricolor Star

Cameroon's flag is a powerful symbol of unity and Pan-African pride. Adopted on May 20, 1975, it replaced the earlier two-star flag with a single golden star at the center—representing the unity of the formerly separate French and British territories. The vertical tricolor of green, red, and yellow echoes the Pan-African colors shared across the continent.

From Prawns to Independence

The very name "Cameroon" comes from the Portuguese "Rio dos Camarões"—River of Prawns—named for the abundant shrimp in the Wouri River estuary. What began as a European trading post grew into one of Africa's most culturally complex nations, shaped by German colonization, French and British rule, and an enduring spirit of unity.

1472

Portuguese explorer Fernando Po arrives at the Wouri River, marveling at the endless shrimp and naming it "Rio dos Camarões." European traders soon follow, exchanging goods, guns, and sadly, slaves.

1884

Germany establishes the colony of Kamerun, stretching far into the interior. The Germans build railways, plantations, and infrastructure—yet also brutally suppress local resistance, leaving a complex legacy.

1916–1960

After World War I, the territory is divided between France and Britain. French Cameroon becomes independent on January 1, 1960, under President Ahmadou Ahidjo. British Southern Cameroons faces a different path.

October 1, 1961

British Southern Cameroons gains independence and merges with the Republic of Cameroon to form the Federal Republic. Two stars on the original flag represented these two states—East and West Cameroon.

May 20, 1972

A referendum abolishes the federal system, creating the United Republic of Cameroon. The single star replaces two, symbolizing one unified nation. May 20 remains Cameroon's National Day, celebrated with parades and pride across the country.

Today

Modern Cameroon is a nation of 30 million people, blessed with extraordinary biodiversity, two official languages, and an unrivaled cultural richness. From the Waza savannas to the Korup rainforest, it remains one of Africa's most rewarding—if challenging—destinations.

Cities That Shape Cameroon

Each major city in Cameroon feels like a different country. From the political elegance of Yaoundé to the commercial pulse of Douala, the cool highland air of Bamenda to the Sahelian warmth of Garoua—urban Cameroon is a journey in itself.

Political Capital

Yaoundé

Sprawling across seven hills, Yaoundé is one of Africa's most pleasantly laid-out capitals. Visit the Reunification Monument—an architectural masterpiece symbolizing the union of French and British Cameroons. The National Museum offers deep insight into the country's 250+ ethnic groups, while the Notre-Dame des Victoires Cathedral dominates the skyline. Don't miss Mefou National Park nearby, a sanctuary for rescued gorillas and chimpanzees.

Economic Capital

Douala

Cameroon's beating commercial heart sits on the Wouri River delta. Douala is loud, chaotic, and utterly addictive. Explore the Douala Maritime Museum, snap photos of the iconic La Nouvelle Liberté sculpture, and lose yourself in the sensory overload of Eko Market. The Bonanjo district reveals elegant colonial architecture, while the Wouri Bridge offers sunset views over one of Africa's busiest ports.

Highland Jewel

Bamenda

Nestled in the cool Northwest highlands, Bamenda offers a refreshing escape from coastal humidity. The city is the gateway to the legendary Ring Road—one of Africa's most spectacular drives—winding through traditional chiefdoms, volcanic lakes, and the surreal landscapes of the Grassfields. Explore local craft markets, visit the Bafut Palace (a UNESCO tentative site), and experience Anglophone Cameroon's unique culture.

Northern Gateway

Garoua

The largest city in northern Cameroon sits on the Benue River, serving as the gateway to Waza National Park—one of Central Africa's finest wildlife reserves. Garoua's Great Mosque is an architectural landmark, while the nearby Kola Gorges offer dramatic rock formations and hidden waterfalls. The city pulses with Fulani culture, vibrant markets, and the rhythmic heat of the Sahel.

Coastal Paradise

Limbe

Where the Atlantic meets the volcanic slopes of Mount Cameroon, Limbe enchants with black-sand beaches, botanical gardens dating to 1892, and a wildlife rescue center for gorillas and chimps. The Limbe Botanic Garden is one of Africa's oldest, while the nearby Bimbia slave port offers a poignant historical journey. It's the perfect place to unwind after inland adventures.

Nature's Grand Stage

Cameroon doesn't do landscapes by half-measure. You can climb an active volcano at dawn, watch elephants drink at sunset, and stand beneath waterfalls that tumble directly into the ocean—all in a single trip.

People & Culture

Cameroon is a linguistic marvel. French and English are the official languages—a legacy of colonial partition—but walk ten kilometers and you might hear Bamileke, Bamoun, Fulfulde, Ewondo, Bassa, or any of 250+ indigenous tongues. This is a country where a taxi driver might switch between four languages in a single conversation. The food is equally diverse: try ndolé (bitterleaf stew with peanuts and shrimp), grilled fish with plantains, or the fiery pepper soup that clears sinuses and souls alike.

Did You Know?

Cameroon has one of the highest literacy rates in Africa and is a powerhouse of African literature, music, and football. The Indomitable Lions national team was the first African squad to reach the FIFA World Cup quarterfinals in 1990. Writers like Ferdinand Oyono and Mongo Beti shaped Francophone African literature, while musicians such as Manu Dibango (who created the global hit "Soul Makossa") put Cameroonian jazz on the world map.
