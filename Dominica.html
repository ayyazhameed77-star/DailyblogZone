Country Background

Dominica is a sovereign island nation located in the Lesser Antilles, in the eastern Caribbean Sea. The island was originally inhabited by the Kalinago people, who called it "Waitukubuli" meaning "Tall is her body" — a fitting name for an island dominated by soaring volcanic peaks. Today, Dominica is home to the largest remaining Indigenous community in the Caribbean, with the Kalinago Territory covering 3,700 acres across eight villages. citeweb_search:7#5web_search:7#2

The island's colonial history mirrors that of its Caribbean neighbors, with control shifting between France and Britain before ultimately becoming a British colony. Dominica gained Associated Statehood in 1967 and achieved full independence from the United Kingdom on November 3, 1978. The island nation has a population of approximately 74,000 people, with about 15,000 calling the capital city of Roseau home. citeweb_search:7#7web_search:7#0

What makes Dominica truly unique is its extraordinary biodiversity. Rainforests blanket two-thirds of the island, and it is the only country in the world where you can spot sperm whales year-round — over 300 of them call these waters home. The island is also famous for having one of the highest concentrations of centenarians in the world, thanks to its nutrient-rich volcanic soil, active lifestyle, and natural, plant-based diet. citeweb_search:7#4web_search:7#5

The National Flag

The flag of Dominica was designed by renowned artist and playwright Alwin Bully and first raised on Independence Day, November 3, 1978. The original design proved too complex for easy manufacture, leading to three modifications in 1981, 1988, and 1990. The current flag is celebrated worldwide as one of the most distinctive and meaningful national symbols. citeweb_search:7#7web_search:7#8

Symbolism of the Design

The green field represents the island's lush forests and natural wealth. The three-colored cross of yellow, black, and white symbolizes the Holy Trinity and the island's diverse people — yellow for the sunshine and the Kalinago/Arawak Indigenous heritage, white for the purity of the rivers and waterfalls, and black for the rich volcanic soil and African heritage. The red disk at the center represents socialism and social justice. Within it, the Sisserou Parrot (Imperial Amazon) — Dominica's national bird and one of the rarest parrots on Earth — symbolizes flight toward greater heights and the fulfillment of national aspirations. The ten green stars encircling the parrot represent the ten parishes of Dominica, signifying equality and hope. citeweb_search:7#7web_search:7#8

Famous Cities & Must-Visit Spots

From the colorful capital to charming coastal towns, here are the places that define the Dominica experience:

imageimage_search:8#8

Roseau

The vibrant capital built on the site of the ancient Kalinago village of Sairi, with a playful Rastafarian vibe and dancehall music swirling through the air. Stroll the scenic streets lined with colonial French and modern architecture, visit the historic Old Market (the last of its kind in the Caribbean), and explore the beautiful Botanical Gardens with its parrot sanctuary. Don't miss the bustling Saturday market overflowing with tropical fruits, or the stunning Morne Bruce viewpoint overlooking the city and sea. citeweb_search:7#3web_search:7#0

Portsmouth

The island's second-largest town on the northwest coast, once the capital until a malaria outbreak in 1760 saw power shift to Roseau. Today, Portsmouth offers access to the idyllic Prince Rupert Bay and the stunning Cabrits National Park — a UNESCO World Heritage Site featuring historic Fort Shirley, where the most important naval battle in Caribbean history was fought in 1782. Enjoy the Saturday farmers market, first-class cricket at Benjamin's Park, and river cruises along the famous Indian River. citeweb_search:7#3web_search:7#0

Soufrière & Scott's Head

A picturesque village on Dominica's southwest coast, famous as a freediving hotspot and home to the iconic Scott's Head landmark where the Caribbean Sea meets the Atlantic Ocean. Nearby, soak in therapeutic sulphuric hot springs, snorkel at Champagne Reef with its volcanic bubbles rising from the seabed, and explore the vibrant marine life that makes this one of the Caribbean's top diving destinations. citeweb_search:7#2web_search:7#0

Essential Travel Tips

Best Time to Visit: December through May offers the best weather outside the rainy season. For fewer crowds at attractions like Trafalgar Falls and Emerald Pool, visit outside peak cruise season (October to May). citeweb_search:7#4

Must-Do Adventures: Hike to Boiling Lake (the world's second-largest hot lake), canyon down waterfalls with Extreme Dominica, swim at Emerald Pool in Morne Trois Pitons National Park, go whale watching for sperm whales, and snorkel the volcanic bubbles at Champagne Reef. citeweb_search:7#0web_search:7#5

Getting Around: Local buses and taxis serve the main routes, but renting a car gives you the freedom to explore. Be prepared for winding mountain roads and stunning scenery at every turn!

Food to Try: Callaloo soup, cassava cakes, fresh tropical fruits, mountain chicken (local frog), and the legendary Dominican coffee. Don't miss the bush bars for homemade rum infusions with ginger, coconut, and local herbs! citeweb_search:7#2web_search:7#0

Final Thoughts

Dominica is not your typical Caribbean holiday — and that's exactly why you should go. This is an island where you can hike through primeval rainforest, swim in emerald pools beneath waterfalls, soak in volcanic hot springs, and watch sperm whales breach just offshore. It's where the Kalinago people still practice ancient traditions, where rivers are clean enough to drink from, and where the soil is so rich that people regularly live past 100 years.

Pack your hiking boots, bring your sense of wonder, and prepare to fall in love with the wildest, greenest, most soul-stirring island in the Caribbean. Dominica is waiting — and once you experience its raw, untamed beauty, you'll never look at the Caribbean the same way again.

Dènye Pawòl

Dominik pa vakans Karayib tipik ou — épi sé sa ki fè ou dwe alé. Sé yon zile koté ou sa ka monté adan lapli twavay pwémityal, naje an basen emwòd anba falls, benyen an sous chòd vulkanik, épi gade balèn sperm ka soté jis an larg. Sé koté pèp Kalinago ka pratik tradisyon ansyen, koté rivyè pwòp ase pou bwè, épi koté latè rich tèlman moun ka viv souvan pli ki 100 lané.

Pake bòt monté ou, pote lespri wondè ou, épi pwépare pou tonb an lanmou épi zil ki pli sovaj, ki pli vèt, épi ki pli ka touchem san an Karayib. Dominik ka tann ou — épi yon fwa ou eksperyans bèlè sovaj li, ou p'ap janm gade Karayib menm jan anko.
